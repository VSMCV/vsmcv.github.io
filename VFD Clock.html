Vacuum Fluorescent Display Clock

Thanks to their impressive efficiency and reliability, LCD screens have become ubiquitous in consumer electronics. There is however a place where they never excelled at, and that is aesthetics. And in my opinion, even if the scope of a desk clock or a microwave oven is mostly utilitarian, every dreary replacement for an everyday object is a step towards a more boring and depressing home.

The pale glow and poor viewing angles of LCDs have been the norm for so long now that a person of my age might not even know better. In fact, VFD and other aesthetic display tech is so rare these days that I had to design and build this clock all by myself.

Design

VFD is not the easiest kind of display to drive, as it requires voltages upwards of 20V, and even more for multiplexing. A filament driver is also required, at around 1-3V. This clock uses a 40V boost converter of my own design, coupled with a (quite rare) MAX6920 high voltage driver IC in a multiplexed configuration. Despite the fact that an AC driver is recommended for the filaments, DC did not seem to cause uneven illumination in my case. Timekeeping is done by an Olimex RTC evaluation board, as I had no experience with high-precision crystal oscillators. The UI and display control software runs on an XMC 2GO evaluation board, which I use frequently because it's cheap and reasonably powerful. The code is quite comprehensive - it includes automatic DST adjustments and preserves the settings during mains power failures. It even dims the display according to ambient brightness levels in order to increase the lifespan of the tubes. The wooden housing for the two PCBs is built from an IKEA bathroom accesory and I made the holes with the most basic woodworking tools, which was quite painful. Either way, I think it was a useful experience to build a complete "consumer product". I had never been so concerned about safety and overall user experience before.
