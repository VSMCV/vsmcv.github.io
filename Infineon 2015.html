Infineon Robot Building Workshops 2015

The robot building workshops organized by Infineon Romania were probably the most important robotics lessons I've attended. The engineers at Infineon took me from the Blinky Board (the "Hello World!" of electronics) to being able to design and build a competitive line follower robot, all in the span of 6 months. Most importantly, the course was not "on rails", as opposed to many other beginner-oriented ones.

Design

My team of four settled on a single double-sided board design, with the common tricycle configuration - two powered wheels in the back and a bearing ball as the third support. The controller is an Infineon XMC connected to eight (analog but quantized via comparators) line sensors. The motor drivers are two Infineon H-Bridges. The reduction ratio is 30:1 for the micromotors, which we admittedly found a bit large.

As for the software, we used a PD algorithm; we weren't able to make the full PID work propery before the contest. The software was arguably the weakest part of the project - we had no experience tuning the parameters, and we got stuck in a local optimum as none of us thought about trying significant alterations to the numbers. Out team finished fourth. After figuring out a better algorithm, we were able to take part in another contest, where we held our ground against much more experienced roboticists.

2017 Revision

Just before leaving for college in 2017, I remembered that the robot had remained with me. I figured I should give it a proper goodbye, so I set out to implement a Hill Climb algorithm and achieve perfect tuning. This resulted in spotless performance, though I must admit that the base tune which I provided was pretty good in itself. I also made a very surprising discovery during the process: higher sampling frequency resulted in worse performance past some threshold (100 Hz in my case). I suspect it has to do with the way the derivative "D" is calculated. Suppose we have an extremely high sampling frequency. The array of eight sensors obviously has limited resolution. Thus, the error may never change faster than one "error unit". That is, "D" can only take the values 0 and 1. Clearly, that would be very bad.
